These may include homesickness, sadness, loneliness, frustration, anxiety, difficulty concentrating, and more.\n\nCulture shock is usually not sudden or "shocking." Instead, it describes a continuum of feelings. Usually, there is a period of excitement when you first arrive--there is so much to see and do! The more negative feelings associated with culture shock, such as anxiety, frustration, or loneliness, typically come soon after. The good news is that culture shock is temporary<<if $shortterm>>, and when you are on a short-term program you may not experience it at all.<<else>>. Nearly all students will, after a few weeks, adjust to and thrive in their new surroundings.<</if>>\n\nHow long culture shock lasts and how difficult it is, of course, differs depending on the person. <<sidebar "Religious identity abroad">>\nReligion is a prominent feature of cultures around the world, even in countries that are officially atheist. !Passport and Document Security\n!!!!Keep your passport and other key documents safe\nWhen you are abroad, no single document is more important than your passport. Without it, ''you will not be able to cross international borders'', and other tasks--such as transferring funds--may also become difficult or impossible. ''Vaccination records, particularly your COVID-19 vaccination record,'' deserve the same care. Take the original with you overseas, but also take a photocopy of these documents to carry around with you when you are out.\n\nWe also recommend keeping a scan of your passport information page, vaccination records, credit cards, and other important documents on a ''cloud service such as Google Drive or iCloud''. !Money\n!!!!Use debit and credit cards wisely to save money\nThe amount of money you spend while studying abroad will vary depending on the local cost of living, your personal spending habits, independent travel, and so on. //How you access your money// can also play a role in maintaining your budget.\n\nMany businesses will not accept plastic, especially for smaller purchases, so it's a good idea to have ''cash'' on hand throughout your time abroad. The most cost-effective way to get cash abroad is to use your ''debit card'' (not a credit card!) in an ATM. You can usually find out where the ATMs you might need are before you leave the US--ask your bank how.\n\nGEO also recommends that you bring a ''credit card''. Be aware that every credit card purchase or debit card withdrawal will likely carry transaction fees. Shop around a bit for the best rates before you leave the US, but also minimize the number of times you use your cards. Take out as much cash as you feel comfortable with each time you use the ATM, and use your credit card only when necessary or when you have an especially large purchase.\n\nSome merchants will offer to convert your purchase into dollars before your card payment. <<sidebar "Road safety overseas">>\nMovies and media reporting have conditioned us to think that the greatest threats to safety abroad are dramatic, "foreign," and of the "third world." The reality is that the ''number one cause of injury and death for American students abroad is traffic accidents''. And the vast majority of these occur in wealthy, industrialized countries.\n\nGEO suggests that you train yourself to pay maximum attention at all times. Whether walking or driving, avoid earbuds or looking at your phone. These distractions, as well as drinking, can all impair perception! If you have a choice between riding in a taxicab or on the roof of a crowded bus--choose the cab! <<sidebar "Coping with stress & culture shock">>\nCulture shock is one expression of stress, but even after culture shock passes, living and studying abroad may sometimes be stressful. Stress may be harder to manage when you are far from your normal support group of family and friends. Learn to recognize the signs of stress and take steps to cope with the situation and make yourself more resilient.\n\n|box|k\n|>|>|Common signs of stress|h\n|exhaustion|fatigue|change in appetite|\n|anxiety|isolation|alienation|\n|frustration|extreme homesickness|increased use of nicotine, alcohol, or drugs|\n|loneliness|change in behavior|self-harming|\n|>|>|new or more frequent physical symptoms, e.g. headaches, stomachaches, etc.|\n\nOne of the best ways to combat stress in a new environment is to prepare for the new location. <<sidebar "Transgender travelers">>\nTransgender students are valued members of the Vanderbilt community. Abroad, attitudes regarding transgender students may not be the same. !Before you leave\n!!!!Know where you are medically before you depart\nRegardless of whether you have an existing medical condition, there are a few things you should do before you go abroad. <<sidebar "More on credit cards">>\nEurope and much of the rest of the world have long used chip-and-PIN “smart” credit cards. These cards offer more secure transactions by adding an embedded microchip and replacing easily forged signatures with entry of a PIN number.\n\nWhile US cards all now have chips, a small proportion of overseas processing machines may not be compatible with the US version of the technology. To maximize payment convenience, ''learn the PIN numbers for any credit cards //and// debit cards'' you bring with you.\n\n''Self-service kiosks are the main culprit for difficulties,'' and may not work even if you do know your US-issued card’s PIN. If you are traveling via train or bus, plan to arrive a little early so that you can find a cashier if need be, and carry cash as well so that you have an alternate form of payment if required. Other places self-service machines might be required are at luggage lockers, bike-rental kiosks, highway tolls, etc.\n\nIf you are using a credit card, be sure that you are making a simple payment rather than a cash advance. Cash advances can result in high transaction and interest payments.\n\nYou may want to ''shop around for banks and credit cards'' before you leave. Seek out cards that carry low foreign transaction fees, as well as debit card providers with overseas partnerships that allow for lower ATM fees.\n\n<<segue "Where will you live?">>

<<sidebar "How to get a travel visa">>\nA ''travel visa'' is a stamp or sticker placed in your passport that allows you to enter a foreign country. Don't forget to keep your family in the loop; if there is an emergency back home, they will want to know how get in touch with you.\n<div class="talkbox">\n|box|k\n|!COVID-19 Considerations|\n|You cannot count on being able to travel to other countries independently while you are on your program. There may be restrictions on non-essential travel between countries, even within normally open regional blocs like the EU. New restrictions could be put in place at any time, including while you are traveling! Your program may also impose restrictions out of an abundance of caution.|\n|Even if travel is possible, it may be more difficult than before. Before you set out, be sure that you understand requirements to enter other countries as well as requirements to return to your host country. Always carry your essential travel documents when you travel, including passport and proof of vaccination!|\n</div>\nSome countries that you plan to visit may have different entry requirements than your primary host country. ''Investigate all the countries you're interested in'' before you leave the US. If any require a <span title="A stamp or sticker placed in your passport that allows you to enter a foreign country.">travel visa</span>, it will probably be easier to get the visa while you're in the US--this applies to both international students and US citizens!\n\nIn every country you visit, make a point of knowing where your ''home country's nearest embassy or consulate'' is. You want to know where you can find support if you get into real trouble. (If you're a US citizen, you should bookmark the [[list of US embassies around the world|http://www.usembassy.gov/]].)\n\nAs you make travel plans, consider the ''ecological impact'' of your travel. Per person, [[airplane travel|https://www.theguardian.com/environment/ng-interactive/2019/jul/19/carbon-calculator-how-taking-one-flight-emits-as-much-as-many-people-do-in-a-year]] generally emits far more carbon dioxide into the atmosphere than travel by train, bus, or automobile. Think about whether you really need to leave your host city so often, and about whether you can use alternative transport that would reduce your carbon footprint.\n\nFinally, independent travel is one of the ''most expensive activities'' you can undertake abroad. Be sure to budget appropriately, and remember to consider safety while you're on the lookout for ways to travel on the cheap.\n<<if not $facultyled>><div class="talkbox">\n|box|k\n|!Wisdom from alums|\n|Every year, students tell us that while they loved the traveling they did abroad, they also regretted not getting to know their host city better. !Personal emergencies\n!!!!What to do after experiencing a personal emergency\nPersonal emergencies may arise from illness, accidents, behavioral health crises, or criminal/negligent behavior. They generally require immediate assistance, whether from a health professional, law enforcement, a counselor, or some combination of these. If you encounter a personal emergency abroad, follow these steps:\n\n''Request help immediately''. Know the local [[emergency number or numbers|$local911URL]] (911 doesn't work abroad!) and have your phone with you at all times. You may download GEO's [[wallet-sized emergency numbers directory|$emergencyCardURL]] to record information in a handy, portable format.\n\n''Notify your program's emergency contact''. <<if $facultyled>>Your faculty leader or another designated person <<else>>On most programs, at least one member of your program's staff <</if>>will be available to you around the clock. On-site personnel are best positioned to provide timely and appropriate emergency support. If you are reporting an emergency on behalf of a friend, be sure to identify yourself as well as your friend.\n\nIf you are in immediate danger, ''contact law enforcement as soon as possible''. If you are considering reporting something less immediate to law enforcement, contact your <<if $facultyled>>faculty leader or other designated contact <<else>>program staff <</if>>first. They may be in a better position to talk with law enforcement<<if not $facultyled>>, and will also know the proper protocols<</if>>. Work with them if at all possible. If you do not have access to resident program staff, contact Vanderbilt Incident Response for Travel (VIRT) at +1 615-322-2745.\n\n''Contact your insurance provider'' if the emergency requires medical attention of any kind. This may be Vanderbilt's international health insurance provider (GeoBlue) or a provider utlized by your program abroad.\n\n''Notify Vanderbilt Incident Response for Travel'' (VIRT). [[VIRT|http://www.vanderbilt.edu/global/virt]] is Vanderbilt's 24/7 emergency response service. They can help you coordinate support services and get you the help you need.\n\n''Contact the nearest embassy''. Consular agents at US embassies are available 24/7 to help US citizens with emergencies. If you are not a US citizen, contact both the US embassy (tell them you are a student at a US university) and your home country's embassy.\n\n''Contact your family''. Your family will be anxious and concerned about your well-being. Your program staff <<if not $facultyled>>and GEO are both<<else>>are<</if>> limited in the information they can share, so be sure to communicate with your family directly if you can.\n\n<<segue "Large-scale emergencies">>\s

<<sidebar "Gifts and relationships">>\nPresenting a gift shows that you value a relationship--even a new one. Consider a gift to start off your more important relationships abroad, such as with your homestay family or a host-country roommate.\n\nIn a cross-cultural setting, the best gifts can be those that let you bring a little of where you're from to the relationship. Examples might include a Vanderbilt mug or pendant, a photo book about Nashville or your hometown, maple syrup if you're from Vermont, or maybe biscuit or grits mix if you're from the South. Not only are gifts like these polite, positive, well-meaning gestures, they can also spur conversations ("Is an American biscuit the same thing as a scone?").\n\nPhotos of home are another great conversation starter. Make sure you bring along some photos of your family, your house, your campus, your hobbies, and so on. It's a great way for people to feel like they are getting to know you and where you come from. \n\n<<segue "Before you leave">>\s
<<sidebar "Communicating with home">>\nYou will need to strike an appropriate balance between the folks back home and your <<if $shortterm>>time<<else>>new life<</if>> abroad. Your family wants to know you are safe and having a good time, and your friends want to keep in touch--so don’t cut them off completely!\n\nAt the same time, be careful not to spend //all// your time chatting back home. Constant contact might be tempting, but it will ultimately make culture shock harder to bear and prevent you from experiencing your host country to the fullest. Our suggestion for keeping a healthy balance: get out and explore, make new friends, and set up a weekly schedule for contacting home.\n\nAlso keep your experiences in perspective when you call home. What you report from abroad can seem much more serious to family who aren't around to assess the situation for themselves. Beware of venting in particular: we have seen many instances where families interpreted momentary frustration as a dire problem. This is rarely productive! Work with your on-site staff to remedy problems, and make sure your family knows you are safe and happy.\n\n<<segue "Disabilities and accommodations">>
